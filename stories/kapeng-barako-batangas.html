Kapeng barako — the liberica variety that once made Batangas synonymous with Philippine coffee — is disappearing acre by acre. What's left is a small, stubborn network of family roasters still processing it the way their grandparents did.

Farmers have been quietly converting barako plots to calamansi and dragon fruit for a decade now, chasing faster, more reliable returns. The beans that remain are sun-dried on bamboo mats, roasted in small batches over wood or charcoal, and ground fresh only when a customer actually shows up. It's slow, inefficient by commercial standards, and exactly why the flavor hasn't been engineered out of it.

Background

At a roastery outside Lipa, third-generation roaster Bert Ilagan still gauges doneness by the smell and the sound of the beans cracking — no thermometer, no timer. He estimates barako now accounts for a small fraction of Batangas's coffee output, down sharply from what he says was closer to a third when his grandfather started roasting.

There's a modest revival underway, driven mostly by specialty cafés in Manila willing to pay a premium for traceable, small-batch liberica. Whether that's enough to reverse decades of conversion to other crops is still an open question — but for now, the roasters are still roasting.

"No thermometer, no timer. You roast it until it smells like it's ready — the same way my grandfather taught me."

It became clear, standing in that roastery, that what looked like inefficiency from the outside was actually the entire point. Barako survives on farms and in kitchens that never optimized it — and the moment someone tries to speed it up, the flavor that makes it worth saving disappears with the shortcut.

For now, a cup of real kapeng barako still exists — you just have to know which roastery to ask for it.
