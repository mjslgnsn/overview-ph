Somewhere between the last milk tea rebrand and this year’s third iced coffee opening, a pale, grassy green started showing up on every feed — Manila, Cebu, and yes, sleepy provincial capitals like Balanga, Bataan. It wasn’t coffee. It was matcha, whisked, layered, and photographed within an inch of its life.

Matcha isn’t new to the Philippines — Japanese cafés and Starbucks seasonal menus have carried it for years. What’s new is the scale: dedicated matcha bars are opening in strip malls and converted bungalows nationwide, and the drink is increasingly outselling coffee at some local chains during peak hours. Food writers trace the current wave to a 2025 resurgence, fueled largely by Gen Z’s interest in wellness — and, admittedly, the aesthetic that comes with a pale-green drink.

Why Gen Z Fell for a 900-Year-Old Tea

For Gen Z drinkers especially, matcha has become both a caffeine alternative and an aesthetic signal — a quieter kind of stimulant than a triple-shot espresso, and one that photographs better besides. Global matcha production is estimated to have grown by nearly 139% over three years, and locally, the drink has reportedly started to eclipse the milk tea craze that defined the previous decade of Filipino café culture.

Part of it is genuinely about wellness — antioxidants, a gentler caffeine curve, no sugar crash. But talk to enough regulars and the ritual matters just as much as the ingredient: the slow whisking, the specific bowl, the pause before the first sip. It’s a small, exportable piece of Japanese tea culture that fits neatly into a generation that already treats journaling and skincare as forms of self-care.

The Café Aesthetic

“It’s become both a caffeine alternative and an aesthetic signal.”

That’s really the turning point in understanding this trend: matcha didn’t win because it tastes nothing like coffee. It won because it comes with a built-in visual identity — the color, the ceramic, the slow whisk — that a cup of brewed coffee simply doesn’t offer for free. Café owners figured this out fast, and started designing entire spaces around it.

Walk into any of the new matcha-only spots in Quezon City, Poblacion, or Bacolod and the room tells you the same story before the drink even arrives: pale wood, terracotta tiles, a single shelf of ceremonial-grade tins, and exactly one wall built for a photo. It’s café design as content strategy — and it’s working.

On TikTok and Instagram, a shop’s matcha isn’t just a menu item anymore; it’s a recurring character in someone’s feed.

Key Takeaways

Gen Z's wellness focus, not just flavor, is what’s driving matcha past milk tea as the go-to café order. Matcha comes with a built-in visual identity — color, ceramic, ritual — that cafés are designing entire spaces around. Social media and local celebrity endorsements have turned matcha into recurring content, not just a menu item. And the trend has moved past Metro Manila: small-town cafés, Bataan included, are opening matcha-first menus of their own.
